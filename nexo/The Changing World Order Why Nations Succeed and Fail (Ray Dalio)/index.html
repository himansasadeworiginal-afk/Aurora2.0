The Premise

Three Big Forces Are Converging

For the first time in our lifetimes, three massive cycles are peaking simultaneously — just as they did in 1930-45.

1. The Long-Term Debt Cycle: Interest rates are at or near 0%, debt is at all-time highs, and central banks have exhausted their traditional tools. Printing money is the only option left — and it devalues currencies.

2. The Domestic Wealth & Power Cycle: Wealth, values, and political gaps are wider than at any time in our lifetimes. Economic downturns will trigger intense conflict over how to divide the shrinking pie.

3. The International Wealth & Power Cycle: For the first time in his lifetime, Dalio sees the US encountering a true rival — China — challengning it in trade, technology, geopolitics, and capital.

The Archetype

The Big Cycle of Empires

Every empire follows the same three-phase arc: Ascent → Top → Decline. Click each phase to expand.

Phase 1: Ascent

50–100 Years of Building
Strong education, character, and work ethic → competitiveness → income growth → investment → new technologies → world trade → military strength → financial center → reserve currency.

Phase 2: Top

Seeds of Decline Grow Within Success
People become expensive → copied by competitors → decadence sets in → over-borrowing (reserve currency privilege) → empire becomes uneconomical → wealth gaps widen.

Phase 3: Decline

Self-Reinforcing Downward Spiral
Debt crisis → printing money → wealth gaps → internal conflict → capital flight → rival powers challenge → revolution/war → destruction → restructuring → new order.

The 8 Measures

How Power Is Measured

Dalio created an index of total power from eight composite measures. They rise and fall in a specific order.

📚

Education

The long-leading indicator. Quality of education predicts future competitiveness and innovation. It's the first to rise and the first to decline.

🏆

Competitiveness

How cost-effective and productive a country's workers are relative to global peers. Success breeds higher costs, which erodes competitiveness.

💡

Technology

Innovation drives both commercial and military advantage. New technologies create new industries and disrupt existing power structures.

🏭

Economic Output

Share of global production. The largest economy typically has the most resources to invest in military, infrastructure, and R&D.

🌍

World Trade Share

Dominance in global trade requires a strong military to protect routes, and a strong financial system to facilitate transactions.

⚔️

Military Strength

Needed to protect trade, enforce influence, and project power. Expensive to maintain — especially when empire becomes overextended.

🏦

Financial Center

The world's leading financial center (Amsterdam → London → New York → Shanghai?) attracts and distributes global capital.

💰

Reserve Currency

The long-lagging indicator. Last to rise, last to fall. Gives "exorbitant privilege" — but always, eventually, every reserve currency dies.

Key insight: Education rises first, reserve currency falls last. The US still has dollar dominance, but its education and competitiveness have been declining for decades. History says the dollar will eventually follow — the question is when and how.

Case Studies

The Rise & Fall of Empires

Four empires that followed the same archetypical cycle. Click to expand each story.

1600s Peak

Dutch Empire

A small country became the world's greatest empire through trade, shipping, and finance.
The Dutch created the Dutch East India Company (the first multinational corporation) and Amsterdam became the world's financial center. The guilder was the first reserve currency. Decline came from becoming too expensive, being copied by the British, and overextending militarily. The Anglo-Dutch wars and financial bubbles accelerated the decline.

1800s Peak

British Empire

The Industrial Revolution gave Britain a decisive edge in production, trade, and military power.
London became the world's financial center, the pound the reserve currency. The Royal Navy ruled the seas. Decline: lost American colonies, faced German/Japanese challenges, took on enormous debt in WWI and WWII, colonies became uneconomical. By 1945, Britain was bankrupt and the US took over as the dominant power.
+ Expand
1945 — Present

American Empire

After WWII, the US had 80% of the world's gold, half of global production, and a nuclear monopoly.
Bretton Woods (1944) made the dollar the reserve currency. The US built the post-war world order (UN, World Bank, IMF, NATO). Decades of peace and prosperity followed. Now: extreme debt, wealth gaps, political polarization, overextended empire, and a rising China challenger. Classic late-cycle position.
+ Expand
600 AD — Present

Chinese Empire

Dominant for centuries, then declined, and is now rising back toward its historical position.
China was the world's leading power for most of history before 1800. Then came the "Century of Humiliation" (1800-1949). Under Mao (1949-76): foundation building. Deng (1978-2013): reforms, opening up, rapid growth. Xi (2013-present): becoming a world power. Now challenging the US in every domain. History suggests this conflict is natural and predictable.

US-China

The Seven Wars

The US and China are now engaged in conflicts across seven domains simultaneously.

📦

Trade/Economic War

Tariffs, supply chain decoupling, export controls. Both sides trying to reduce dependence on the other.

💻

Technology War

5G, AI, semiconductors, software bans. Who controls the next generation of technology will control the future.

🌏

Geopolitical War

South China Sea, alliances (Quad, AUKUS vs. Belt & Road), influence in developing nations.

🏦

Capital War

Sanctions, currency competition, capital controls. China is pushing to internationalize the yuan.

⚔️

Military War

Show-of-force operations, proxy conflicts, Taiwan tensions, military buildup on both sides.

🎭

Culture War

Ideology, values, propaganda, soft power competition. Each side presents its system as superior.

🪞

War with Ourselves

"The enemy is us." Each country's internal divisions are its greatest vulnerability. A house divided cannot stand.

Where We Are

Where the US Is in Its Big Cycle

Dalio's assessment: The US is in the late stage of its cycle — with declining relative power, extreme debt, and internal conflict. China is in its ascent phase.

The current confluence: The last major destruction/restructuring period was 1930-45. That created a new world order in 1945. It is now 75+ years later — classically near the end of a long-term debt cycle. We have high debt, near-zero rates, large wealth gaps, political polarization, a rising challenger, and a pandemic. This exact confluence happened in 1930-45 — and before that in similar periods.

Principles

How to Navigate What's Coming

Practical takeaways from Dalio's study of 500+ years of history.

1

Know Where You Are in the Cycle

Most people miss big changes because they've never experienced them before. Study history to see the patterns.

2

Diversify Across Countries & Currencies

Don't bet everything on one country or one currency. Reserve currencies always decline eventually.

3

Don't Confuse the Recent Past with the Future

The last 40 years of declining inflation and rising peace were not normal — they were one phase of a long cycle.

4

Watch Leading Indicators

Education quality, debt levels, infrastructure investment, and wealth gaps predict where a country is headed.

5

Invest in Adaptability

Humanity's power to adapt and invent always wins in the long run. Bad periods don't last forever.

6

Expect Radical Change

The next 10-20 years will be radically different from what we've experienced. Prepare mentally and financially.

The Four Laws of Behavior Change

Every habit follows a four-step feedback loop. Master these levers and you can reshape any behavior.

👁️

Make It Obvious

The 1st Law: Cue — Design your environment, use implementation intentions.

🔥

The 2nd Law: Craving — Temptation bundle, join the right culture.

⚡

Make It Easy

The 3rd Law: Response — Two-minute rule, reduce friction, commit.

🎉

The 4th Law: Reward — Track habits, use immediate reinforcement.

Law 1

Make It Obvious

Implementation intentions, habit stacking, environment design.

To break: Make It Invisible — reduce exposure to cues.
Law 2

Make It Attractive

Temptation bundling, join a culture, create rituals.

To break: Make It Unattractive — reframe your mindset.
Law 3

Make It Easy

Two-minute rule, reduce friction, automate decisions.

To break: Make It Difficult — increase friction, use commitment devices.
Law 4

Make It Satisfying

Habit tracking, immediate rewards, never miss twice.

To break: Make It Unsatisfying — add immediate cost or accountability.

Key Concepts

The Big Ideas

The most powerful concepts from the book, each with practical application.

1

Identity-Based Habits

Outcome-based: "I want to lose weight." Identity-based: "I am the kind of person who moves every day." Every action is a vote for the person you wish to become. True change is identity change.

2

The Habit Loop

Cue → Craving → Response → Reward. Every habit follows this cycle. Master each stage and you can design any behavior. The Four Laws are your levers on this loop.

3

Motion vs. Action

Motion = planning, strategizing, learning. Action = producing results. We use motion to delay failure. The best approach? Stop preparing and start doing. Repetition, not perfection.

4

The Two-Minute Rule

A new habit should take less than two minutes. "Read before bed" becomes "Read one page." "Run 5K" becomes "Tie your shoes." Standardize before you optimize. Master the first two minutes.

5

Never Miss Twice

Missing once is an accident. Missing twice is the start of a new bad habit. The rule: get back on track immediately. Perfection is impossible; consistency is everything.

6

Environment Design

Willpower is a short-term strategy. Design your environment so good habits are obvious and bad habits are impossible. Put the fruit on the counter, the guitar by the couch, the phone in another room.

7

Goldilocks Rule

Peak motivation occurs at the edge of your ability — not too hard, not too easy. Boredom is the greatest threat to success. Professionals stick to the schedule even when it's not exciting.

8

Habit Stacking

"After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]." Stack new behaviors onto existing routines. Your morning coffee becomes the cue for 2 minutes of meditation. Your commute becomes your audiobook time.

Tactics

Put It Into Practice

Actionable strategies you can start using today.

  • ✓
    Implementation IntentionsState exactly when and where you'll act: "I will exercise at 7 AM in my living room." Specificity makes the cue obvious.
  • ✓
    Temptation BundlingPair something you want to do with something you need to do: listen to your favorite podcast only while at the gym.
  • ✓
    Reduce FrictionMake good habits effortless. Lay out workout clothes the night before. Pre-pack your lunch. The less friction, the more likely you'll act.
  • ✓
    Commitment DevicesLock in good behavior ahead of time. Buy a timed outlet for your router. Leave your wallet at home. Pre-pay for gym sessions.
  • ✓
    Habit TrackingUse a calendar. Mark an X each day you follow through. Don't break the chain. Visual progress is inherently satisfying and motivating.
  • ✓
    Reflection & ReviewSchedule annual, monthly, and daily reviews. Measure what matters. Without reflection, habits stagnate. Make small adjustments to keep improving.

Government Intervention
in Markets

Understanding how governments intervene through price controls, taxes, subsidies, and supporting policies to correct market outcomes.

↓
⚖️

Price Control

Governments intervene in markets when they perceive the market equilibrium price as unfair or socially undesirable. The two main types of price control are:

🟢 Maximum Price (Ceiling)
  • Legal price set below equilibrium
  • Also called a price ceiling
  • Only effective when set below market equilibrium
  • Aims to protect consumers from high prices
🔴 Minimum Price (Floor)
  • Legal price set above equilibrium
  • Also called a price floor
  • Only effective when set above market equilibrium
  • Aims to protect producers from low prices
📭

Maximum Price (Ceiling Price)

A legal maximum price set below the equilibrium price. At this price, the quantity demanded exceeds the quantity supplied, creating a shortage.

Effects of Maximum Price

⚠️
Shortage
Excess demand: Qd > Qs at the ceiling price
🎵
Black Market
Illegal trading at prices above the ceiling
💰
Hidden Supply
Suppliers may hide goods to sell at higher prices
📉
Welfare Loss
Reduction in total economic surplus

Surplus Analysis: Before vs After

Component Before Ceiling After Ceiling Change
Consumer Surplus A + B A + C +C − B
Producer Surplus C + D + E E −(C + D)
Economic Surplus A + B + C + D + E A + C + E −(B + D)

Black Market Price Calculation

In a black market, goods are sold illegally at prices above the ceiling. The black market price is determined by the quantity supplied at the ceiling price and the demand curve.

Black Market Price = Price at which Qsupplied = Qdemanded on the original demand curve

Remedies for Shortage

🌍
Import
Import goods from abroad to increase supply
📈
Producer Incentives
Subsidies or tax breaks to encourage domestic production
🎯
Rationing
Ration cards, queueing, bribes, or connecting goods to allocate scarce supply
📍 Step-by-Step: Maximum Price Effects
1
Government sets a ceiling price Pc below equilibrium Pe
2
At Pc, Qd > Qs → shortage of size (Qd − Qs)
3
Transaction quantity falls from Qe to Qs (supply constrained)
4
Consumer surplus: some consumers gain (lower price), others lose (cannot buy) → net ambiguous change
5
Producer surplus decreases → producers sell less at a lower price
6
Deadweight loss = area B + D → economic surplus decreases
📑

Minimum Price (Floor Price)

A legal minimum price set above the equilibrium price. At this price, the quantity supplied exceeds the quantity demanded, creating excess supply (surplus).

Effects of Minimum Price

📦
Excess Supply
Qs > Qd at the floor price
🙅‍♂️
Unemployment
In labour markets: minimum wage → job losses
💵
Excess Investment
Firms over-invest due to guaranteed higher price
📉
Welfare Loss
Deadweight loss from reduced transactions

Surplus Analysis: Before vs After

Component Before Floor After Floor Change
Consumer Surplus A + B + C A −(B + C)
Producer Surplus D + E B + D +B − E
Economic Surplus A + B + C + D + E A + B + D −(C + E)

Remedies for Excess Supply

📦
Storage
Government stores the surplus for future use
🔄
By-Products
Convert surplus into other usable products
📈
Demand Promotion
Advertising campaigns to boost consumption
🌍
Exports
Sell surplus in international markets

Maximum vs Minimum Price

🟢 Maximum Price
  • Set below equilibrium
  • Creates shortage (excess demand)
  • Benefits consumers
  • Leads to black markets
  • Remedies: import, rationing, incentives
🔴 Minimum Price
  • Set above equilibrium
  • Creates surplus (excess supply)
  • Benefits producers
  • Leads to over-investment
  • Remedies: storage, exports, promotion
📍 Step-by-Step: Minimum Price Effects
1
Government sets a floor price Pf above equilibrium Pe
2
At Pf, Qs > Qd → excess supply of size (Qs − Qd)
3
Transaction quantity falls from Qe to Qd (demand constrained)
4
Consumers buy less at a higher price → consumer surplus decreases
5
Producers sell less but at higher price → net change ambiguous depending on elasticities
6
Deadweight loss = area C + E → economic surplus decreases
💶

Price Supporting Policies

Two main methods governments use to support prices (especially in agriculture):

💵 Government Purchases
  • Government buys excess supply at the minimum price
  • Effectively removes surplus from the market
  • Requires government storage or disposal
  • Cost to government = (Pmin × surplus quantity)
💳 Deficiency Payment System
  • Government pays producers the difference between the minimum price and what consumers actually pay
  • Consumers pay the market-clearing price
  • Producers receive the target (minimum) price
  • Cost to government = (Pmin − Pmarket) × Q
📍 Deficiency Payment Walkthrough
1
Target price (Pmin) is set above equilibrium
2
Consumers pay market-clearing price Pm (where demand meets supply at Q produced)
3
Government pays producers the deficiency: (Pmin − Pm) × Q
4
Producers receive total revenue = (Pm × Q) + deficiency payment = Pmin × Q
💰

Unit Tax

A unit tax is a fixed tax per unit of a good imposed on producers. It shifts the supply curve upward (leftward) by the amount of the tax.

The New Supply Equation

Qs = a + b(P − t)

Where t = tax per unit. For any given market price P, producers only receive (P − t), so they supply less.

Tax Burden: Who Pays More?

The distribution of the tax burden depends on elasticity:

📉 Inelastic Demand
  • Consumers bear more of the tax
  • Price rises significantly → consumer price ↑
  • Example: necessities, petrol, cigarettes
  • Consumer share = ΔP / t (larger)
📈 Elastic Demand
  • Producers bear more of the tax
  • Price rises little → producer price ↓
  • Example: luxury goods, non-essentials
  • Producer share = 1 − (ΔP / t) (larger)
Consumer burden
Inelastic →
Producer burden
← Elastic

Surplus Analysis with Tax

Component Before Tax After Tax Change
Consumer Surplus A + B + C A −(B + C)
Producer Surplus D + E + F F −(D + E)
Government Revenue — B + D +(B + D)
Economic Surplus A + B + C + D + E + F A + B + D + F −(C + E)
📍 Step-by-Step: Unit Tax Effects
1
Tax of t per unit imposed on producers → supply shifts up by t
2
New equilibrium: quantity falls, consumer price rises, producer price falls
3
Consumer pays: Pc = original Pe + ΔPc
4
Producer receives: Pp = Pc − t = original Pe − ΔPp
5
Government revenue = t × Qnew (area B + D)
6
Deadweight loss = area C + E (transactions that no longer occur)
💸

Unit Subsidy

A unit subsidy is a payment per unit of a good from the government to producers (a "negative tax"). It shifts the supply curve downward (rightward) by the amount of the subsidy.

The New Supply Equation

Qs = a + b(P + s)

Where s = subsidy per unit. For any given market price P, producers receive (P + s), so they supply more.

Subsidy Benefit: Who Gains More?

📉 Inelastic Demand
  • Producers capture more of the subsidy
  • Price falls little → producers keep most of it
  • Consumer share = ΔP / s (smaller)
📈 Elastic Demand
  • Consumers capture more of the subsidy
  • Price falls significantly → consumers benefit
  • Consumer share = ΔP / s (larger)

Surplus Analysis with Subsidy

Component Before Subsidy After Subsidy Change
Consumer Surplus A A + B + C +(B + C)
Producer Surplus F D + E + F +(D + E)
Government Cost — B + C + D + E + G −(B + C + D + E + G)
Economic Surplus A + F A + B + C + D + E + F −G
📍 Step-by-Step: Unit Subsidy Effects
1
Subsidy of s per unit given to producers → supply shifts down by s
2
New equilibrium: quantity rises, consumer price falls
3
Consumer price falls by less than s (unless demand is perfectly elastic)
4
Producer receives: Pp = Pc + s (more than before)
5
Cost to government = s × Qnew (large area)
6
Deadweight loss = area G (overproduction beyond efficient output)
⚖️

Tax vs Subsidy Comparison

💰 Unit Tax
  • Supply shifts up
  • Quantity falls
  • Consumer price ↑
  • Producer price ↓
  • Gov revenue +
  • Surplus −
  • DWL: underproduction
💸 Unit Subsidy
  • Supply shifts down
  • Quantity rises
  • Consumer price ↓
  • Producer price ↑
  • Gov cost −
  • Surplus −
  • DWL: overproduction
📊 Key Insight
  • Both create DWL
  • Burden/benefit split depends on elasticity
  • Inelastic side bears more tax / captures more subsidy
  • Both reduce economic efficiency

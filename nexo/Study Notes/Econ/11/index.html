Economic Growth

Economic growth refers to the continuous increase in the real Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of a country over time. It represents the expansion of an economy's production capacity.

How Economic Growth is Shown

📈
PPF Outward Shift
Production Possibility Frontier shifts outward, showing increased capacity to produce goods and services
→
LRAS Rightward Shift
Long-Run Aggregate Supply curve shifts right, indicating sustainable growth in productive capacity

Measuring Economic Growth

Economic Growth Rate = (Current GDP - Previous GDP) / Previous GDP × 100

Worked Example 1

If a country's GDP was $500 billion in 2023 and $525 billion in 2024:

Growth Rate = (525 - 500) / 500 × 100 = 25 / 500 × 100 = 5%

Worked Example 2

If GDP was $1.2 trillion in 2023 and $1.35 trillion in 2024:

Growth Rate = (1.35 - 1.2) / 1.2 × 100 = 0.15 / 1.2 × 100 = 12.5%

Importance & Costs of Economic Growth

✅ Importance
  • Improved living standards & quality of life
  • Increased employment opportunities
  • Successful fiscal management (higher tax revenue)
  • Stronger investments in infrastructure & public goods
  • Increased profits & business trustworthiness
⚠️ Costs
  • Environmental problems, degradation, pollution
  • Depletion of non-renewable resources
  • Negative externalities (health costs, congestion)
  • Inflationary pressures
  • Income & regional disparities widen
  • Decrease in present consumption (savings for investment)

📈
Structural Change

↓

Lower unemployment, poverty & disparity

Growth vs Development

📈 Economic Growth
  • Quantitative increase in output
  • Measured by GDP/GNP growth rate
  • Narrower concept
  • Can occur without development
  • Short to medium-term focus
📉 Economic Development
  • Qualitative transformation + quantitative growth
  • Measured by HDI, poverty reduction, inequality
  • Broader, multidimensional concept
  • Requires growth but goes beyond it
  • Long-term sustainable focus

Multidimensional Nature

Development encompasses economic, social, political, and cultural dimensions. It is not merely about income but about expanding people's freedoms and capabilities.

Labour Force

The labour force is defined as the sum of employed and unemployed persons within the working-age population.

Labour Force = Employment + Unemployment

Sri Lanka Labour Force Calculation

Step 1: Total Population
The entire population of the country
Step 2: Subtract Child Dependents (<10) & Adult Dependents (>60)
= Active Population (working-age population)
Step 3: Subtract Non-Labour Groups
Housewives + Full-time students + Retired + Disabled + Self-unemployed
Step 4: Labour Force
Active Population − Non-labour groups = Labour Force

Key Ratios

📊
Employment Ratio
Employed ÷ Labour Force × 100
📉
Unemployment Ratio
Unemployed ÷ Labour Force × 100
📈
Labour Force Participation Ratio
Labour Force ÷ Active Population × 100

Worked Example

Suppose a country has:
Total Population = 22,000,000
Child dependents (<10) = 3,500,000
Adult dependents (>60) = 2,500,000
Housewives = 1,200,000
Full-time students = 1,800,000
Retired = 800,000
Disabled = 200,000
Self-unemployed = 0

Active Population = 22,000,000 − 3,500,000 − 2,500,000 = 16,000,000
Labour Force = 16,000,000 − 1,200,000 − 1,800,000 − 800,000 − 200,000 = 12,000,000
If Employed = 10,800,000
 Unemployed = 12,000,000 − 10,800,000 = 1,200,000
 Unemployment Ratio = 1,200,000 / 12,000,000 × 100 = 10%

Poverty

Poverty is a state of deprivation where individuals lack the resources to meet basic needs for a minimally acceptable quality of life.

Types of Poverty

💵 Absolute Poverty
  • Below a fixed minimum subsistence level
  • Cannot afford basic necessities (food, water, shelter)
  • Measured by poverty line (e.g., $2.15/day World Bank)
  • Focuses on survival
📉 Relative Poverty
  • Below the average standard of living in society
  • Cannot participate fully in normal social life
  • Measured as % of median income (e.g., below 60%)
  • Focuses on inequality

Poverty and Economic Growth

Economic growth can reduce poverty through the trickle-down effect, but inclusive growth requires deliberate policies for equitable distribution. Development goes beyond growth to directly address poverty through capability enhancement, social protection, and access to education, healthcare, and productive assets.

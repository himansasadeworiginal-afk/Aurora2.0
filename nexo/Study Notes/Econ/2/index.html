Understanding how buyers and sellers interact to determine prices and quantities in a market economy.

Demand

Demand refers to the various quantities of a good or service that buyers are ready to purchase at alternative prices during a given time period.

Individual vs Market Demand

🧑 Individual Demand
  • Quantity demanded by a single consumer
  • Determinants: P, Pn, Y, T, Ex, O
👥 Market Demand
  • Sum of all individual demands
  • Determinants: P, Pn, Y, T, Ex, N, O
  • N = Number of consumers

Determinants of Demand

💵
P — Price
Price of the good itself
💲
Pn — Related Goods
Substitutes & complements
💰
Y — Income
Consumer purchasing power
🏠
T — Tastes
Preferences & fashions
🌍
Ex — Expectations
Future price expectations
📊
O — Other
Weather, ads, population etc.
👥
N — Population
Only for market demand

Law of Demand

↓
Inverse Relationship
As price ↑, quantity demanded ↓ and vice versa (ceteris paribus)

Reasons for the Law

💸
Substitution Effect
When P ↑, consumers switch to cheaper substitutes
💶
Income Effect
When P ↑, real income ↓, purchasing power ↓

Change in Quantity Demanded vs Change in Demand

↕ Change in Qd
  • Movement along the demand curve
  • Caused by change in own price (P)
  • Extension: P ↓ → Qd ↑
  • Contraction: P ↑ → Qd ↓
↔ Change in Demand
  • Shift of the entire demand curve
  • Caused by non-price factors (Pn, Y, T, Ex, O, N)
  • Increase: curve shifts right
  • Decrease: curve shifts left

Supply

Supply refers to the various quantities of a good or service that producers are willing to supply at alternative prices during a given time period.

Institutional vs Market Supply

🏭 Institutional Supply
  • Quantity supplied by a single firm
👥 Market Supply
  • Sum of all institutional supplies

Law of Supply

↑
Positive Relationship
As price ↑, quantity supplied ↑ and vice versa (ceteris paribus)

Reasons for the Law

💰
Profit Concept
Higher price → higher profit incentive to produce more
↗️
Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost
Higher output requires higher price to cover rising marginal costs
Supply Curve (Law of Supply)
Price
  |
  |    / S
  |   /
P2 |  o---
  | / |
P1 |/  o---
  +------------------ Quantity
     Q1    Q2

S slopes upward: positive relationship
⚖️

Market Equilibrium

At equilibrium, the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied at a particular price where buyers and producers mutually agree.

Conditions for Equilibrium

💵
Demand Price = Supply Price
Buyers and sellers agree on price
📊
Qd = Qs
Quantity demanded = Quantity supplied
❌
Excess Demand = 0
No shortage
❌
Excess Supply = 0
No surplus

Excess Demand & Excess Supply

📈 Excess Demand (Shortage)
  • Price is below equilibrium
  • Qd > Qs
  • Market pressure: Price ↑
📉 Excess Supply (Surplus)
  • Price is above equilibrium
  • Qs > Qd
  • Market pressure: Price ↓
Market Equilibrium
Price
  |
  |        S
  |       /
  |      /   Equilibrium Point (E)
P* |-----X-------
  |    /|   \
  |   / |    \  D
  |  /  |     \
  +------------------ Quantity
     Q*
     
At P*: Qd = Qs = Q*
Above P*: Surplus (Qs > Qd)
Below P*: Shortage (Qd > Qs)

Finding Equilibrium from Equations

Example 1

Qd = 4250 − 25P    Qs = 2000 + 50P
  1. Set Qd = Qs: 4250 − 25P = 2000 + 50P
  2. 4250 − 2000 = 50P + 25P → 2250 = 75P
  3. P* = 30
  4. Substitute: Qd = 4250 − 25(30) = 4250 − 750
  5. Q* = 3500

Example 2

Qd = 30 − 2P    Qs = −6 + 2P
  1. Set Qd = Qs: 30 − 2P = −6 + 2P
  2. 30 + 6 = 2P + 2P → 36 = 4P
  3. P* = 9
  4. Substitute: Qd = 30 − 2(9) = 30 − 18
  5. Q* = 12
💡
Check: Excess = Qd − Qs
If P < P* → Excess Demand (positive) → Price ↑
If P > P* → Excess Supply (negative) → Price ↓
💰

Consumer & Producer Surplus

🧑 Consumer Surplus
  • Difference between maximum price a consumer is willing to pay and the equilibrium price
  • Area below the demand curve and above the price
🏭 Producer Surplus
  • Difference between the equilibrium price and the minimum supply price
  • Area above the supply curve and below the price
Economic Surplus = Consumer Surplus + Producer Surplus

CS = area between demand & price (above P*)
PS = area between supply & price (below P*)

Elasticity of Demand

Elasticity measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to changes in its determinants.

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

PED = %ΔQd / %ΔP

Point Elasticity Formula

Ed = (ΔQd/ΔP) × (P/Qd)

Types of Elasticity

∞
Perfectly Elastic (E = ∞)
Horizontal demand curve; infinite response to price change
↑
Elastic (|E| > 1)
%ΔQd > %ΔP; luxury goods
↔
Unit Elastic (|E| = 1)
%ΔQd = %ΔP
↓
Inelastic (|E| < 1)
%ΔQd < %ΔP; necessities
0
Perfectly Inelastic (E = 0)
Vertical demand curve; no response to price change

Cross Price Elasticity of Demand (XED)

XED = %ΔQd of Good X / %ΔP of Good Y
  • Positive XED → Substitutes (tea & coffee)
  • Negative XED → Complements (cars & petrol)
  • Zero XED → Unrelated goods

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

YED = %ΔQd / %ΔY
  • Positive YED → Normal goods (Y ↑ → Qd ↑)
  • Negative YED → Inferior goods (Y ↑ → Qd ↓)
  • YED > 1 → Luxury goods; 0 < YED < 1 → Necessities

Determinants of Price Elasticity of Demand

🔄
Availability of Substitutes
More substitutes = more elastic
💵
Proportion of Income
Higher proportion = more elastic
📅
Time Period
Longer time = more elastic
🔔
Necessity vs Luxury
Necessities = inelastic; Luxuries = elastic
🪙
Nature of Good
Addictive goods = inelastic
📦
Narrowness of Market
Narrower definition = more elastic

Practical Importance of Price Elasticity

💰 Pricing Decisions
Firms set prices based on elasticity to maximize revenue
🏛️ Government Policy
Taxation: inelastic goods generate more tax revenue
🌍 International Trade
Exchange rate changes affect export/import demand
👥 Consumer Behavior
Understanding how consumers respond to price changes
📄

Price Elasticity of Supply

Measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price.

PES = %ΔQs / %ΔP

Types of Supply Elasticity

∞
Perfectly Elastic (PES = ∞)
Horizontal supply curve
↑
Elastic (PES > 1)
%ΔQs > %ΔP
↔
Unit Elastic (PES = 1)
%ΔQs = %ΔP
↓
Inelastic (PES < 1)
%ΔQs < %ΔP
0
Perfectly Inelastic (PES = 0)
Vertical supply curve; fixed supply (land, unique art)

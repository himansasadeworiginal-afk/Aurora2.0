Foreign Exchange

Foreign exchange is the conversion of one country's currency into another country's currency. It is the backbone of international trade and finance.

Foreign Exchange Market

The institutional structure and arrangements through which foreign exchange is bought, sold, and transferred between countries.

Participants

🏦
Commercial Banks
Primary intermediaries in forex transactions
💱
Forex Brokers
Match buyers and sellers, earn commission
🏭
Business Firms
Importers & exporters needing currency
🏦
Central Bank
Monitors and intervenes in forex market
👥
General Public
Travelers, students, remittance receivers
🏰
Government
Debt repayment, diplomatic payments

Profiteers (Arbitrageurs)

Participants who buy foreign exchange at a low price in one market and sell it at a higher price in another market, profiting from price differences across markets.

Arbitrage: Buy Low → Sell High (across different markets)
💳

Nominal Exchange Rate

The nominal exchange rate is the price of one country's currency expressed in terms of another country's currency. It is the rate at which two currencies can be exchanged.

📇 Direct Quotation
  • Price quotation
  • Foreign currency expressed in terms of domestic currency
  • Ex: US$1 = SLKR 152
  • "How many rupees per dollar?"
📈 Indirect Quotation
  • Volume quotation
  • Domestic currency expressed in terms of foreign currency
  • Ex: SLKR 1 = US$0.0065
  • "How many dollars per rupee?"
⚖️

Demand & Supply of Foreign Exchange

Demand for Foreign Exchange (Dollar)

Dollar is demanded when payments need to be made to foreign entities:

📦
Imports
Paying for foreign goods & services
✈️
Travel
Foreign travel expenses
💵
Debt Repayment
Paying foreign loans & interest
🤝
Transfers
Grants, gifts, aid to abroad

Inverse relationship: As rupee value of dollar ↑, quantity demanded ↓

Supply of Foreign Exchange (Dollar)

Dollar is supplied when receipts come from foreign entities:

📦
Exports
Selling goods & services abroad
💰
Primary Income
Interest, dividends, wages from abroad
🧑‍💰
Secondary Income
Remittances, gifts, grants from abroad

Positive relationship: As rupee value of dollar ↑, quantity supplied ↑

Shift Factors

Demand Curve Shifts
  • ↑ Imports → Demand ↑ (right)
  • ↑ Foreign travel → Demand ↑
  • ↑ Debt repayment → Demand ↑
  • ↑ Transfers abroad → Demand ↑
Supply Curve Shifts
  • ↑ Exports → Supply ↑ (right)
  • ↑ Primary income receipts → Supply ↑
  • ↑ Remittances → Supply ↑
  • ↑ Foreign investment → Supply ↑

Exchange Rate Market Equilibrium

Exchange Rate
(Rs/$)    |
         |   S (Supply of $)
         |    \
         |     \
    E ---|------\-----
         |       \   D (Demand for $)
         |        \
         +----------------- Quantity of $
         0        Q
      Equilibrium where D = S

The market exchange rate is determined by the intersection of demand for and supply of foreign currency. At equilibrium, the quantity demanded equals the quantity supplied.

Exchange Rate Systems

🔒 Fixed
  • Determined by monetary authority
  • Based on gold standard (1890–1920)
  • Stable & predictable
  • Requires large reserves
  • Vulnerable to speculative attacks
📈 Floating
  • Determined by market D & S
  • Automatically balances BOP
  • No reserve requirement
  • Volatile & uncertain
  • Self-correcting mechanism
⚖️ Managed Floating
  • Combination of both systems
  • Central bank intervenes within a range
  • Crawling peg / Dirty float
  • Limited flexibility
  • Most countries use this today
📊

Effective Exchange Rates

📊
NEER
Nominal Effective Exchange Rate
📈
REER
Real Effective Exchange Rate
NEER
  • Weighted average of bilateral nominal exchange rates
  • Weights based on trade share with each partner
  • Measured in nominal terms (not adjusted for inflation)
  • NEER = Σ (wi × Ei)
REER
  • NEER adjusted for inflation differences
  • Measures real competitiveness
  • REER = NEER × (Pdomestic / Pforeign)
  • REER ↑ = loss of competitiveness
NEER = Σ (Trade Weighti × Exchange Ratei)
REER = NEER × (Domestic Price Level / Foreign Price Level)
📉📈

Changes in Exchange Rate

📉 Under Fixed System
  • Devaluation: Official lowering of currency value by monetary authority
  • Revaluation: Official increase in currency value by monetary authority
  • Government/monetary authority makes the decision
📈 Under Floating System
  • Depreciation: Market-driven decrease in currency value
  • Appreciation: Market-driven increase in currency value
  • Determined automatically by demand and supply forces
Devaluation vs Depreciation
  • Devaluation is official/policy-driven
  • Occurs under fixed exchange rate system
  • Announced by central bank/government
Similarity
  • Both result in a currency becoming cheaper relative to foreign currencies
  • Both increase the domestic price of foreign currency
  • Both make exports cheaper and imports more expensive
💵

Effects of Devaluation / Depreciation

📈
Import Prices ↑
Increases domestic price of imports, increases import expenditure
💰
Inflation ↑
Can increase domestic inflation (imported inflation)
💵
Debt Service ↑
Increases foreign debt service payments in local currency
🌍
Tourism ↑
Improves tourism earnings (cheaper for foreigners)
🧑‍💰
Remittances ↑
Improves worker remittances value in local currency
📦
Trade Balance
Discourages imports, encourages exports

Marshall-Lerner Condition

For a devaluation/depreciation to improve the trade balance:

|EX| + |EM| > 1

Where EX = price elasticity of demand for exports, EM = price elasticity of demand for imports. If the sum of elasticities exceeds 1, devaluation improves the trade balance.

📉

Effects of Appreciation / Revaluation

📦
Import Prices ↓
Decreases domestic price of imported goods
📉
Inflation ↓
Decreases inflation rate (if import-dependent economy)
💵
Foreign Debt ↓
Decreases cost of repaying foreign debt in local currency
💰
Debt Value ↓
Decreases foreign debt value expressed in domestic currency

Why do countries intentionally devalue their currency?

📉
Minimize BOP Deficit

🚫

Balance of Payments (BOP)

The Balance of Payments is a systematic statement of all economic transactions between residents of a country and non-residents over a specific period (usually a year).

Key Features

📄
Statement
Records all international transactions
🔄
Double Entry
Every transaction recorded twice (credit & debit)
⚖️
Balances
BOP always balances: net = 0

Recording Conventions

📤 Debit (+)
  • When asset/good/service flows FROM the country
  • Payments to non-residents
  • Examples: imports, foreign travel, debt repayment
📥 Credit (−)
  • When asset/good/service flows TO the country
  • Receipts from non-residents
  • Examples: exports, remittances, foreign investment
Total Debits = Total Credits ⇒ BOP always balances to zero
🏢

Structure of BOP

The Balance of Payments has three main accounts:

💳 Current Account
  • Goods (Trade balance)
  • Services
  • Goods & Services
  • Primary Income
  • Secondary Income
🏛️ Capital Account
  • Non-produced, non-monetary assets
  • Licenses, leases
  • Brand names, patents
  • Royalties
💰 Financial Account
  • Direct investments
  • Portfolio investments
  • Financial derivatives
  • Other investments
  • Foreign reserve assets

Current Account in Detail

📦
Goods Account
Trade balance = Exports − Imports
✈️
Services Account
Transport, travel, financial, telecom, insurance
📊
Goods & Services
Goods balance + Services balance
💰
Primary Income
Interest, wages, dividends from abroad
🤝
Secondary Income
Transfers, remittances, gifts, grants

Worked Examples

Example 1: Trade Balance
Exports = $500M
−
Imports = $700M
=
Trade Balance = −$200M (Deficit)
Example 2: Services Balance
Services Exports = $300M
−
Services Imports = $200M
=
Services Balance = +$100M (Surplus)
Example 3: Goods & Services Balance
Goods Balance = −$200M
+
Services Balance = +$100M
=
G & S Balance = −$100M (Deficit)
Example 4: Current Account Balance
G & S Balance = −$100M
+
Primary Income = +$50M
+
Secondary Income = +$80M
=
Current Account = +$30M (Surplus)
Example 5: Capital Account
Patent sales, licenses, royalties
=
Capital Account = +$10M

Summary

  • Foreign exchange converts one currency into another; the forex market has various participants including banks, brokers, central banks, and the public.
  • The nominal exchange rate can be quoted directly (foreign in domestic terms) or indirectly (domestic in foreign terms).
  • Foreign exchange is demanded for imports, travel, debt repayment, and transfers; it is supplied by exports, primary income, and secondary income.
  • Three main exchange rate systems: fixed (monetary authority), floating (market D & S), and managed floating (hybrid).
  • Devaluation/depreciation makes imports expensive and exports cheap; appreciation/revaluation has the opposite effect.
  • The Marshall-Lerner condition requires |EX| + |EM| > 1 for devaluation to improve the trade balance.
  • BOP records all international transactions via double-entry system, always balancing to zero across current, capital, and financial accounts.

Production Boundary

The production boundary defines which economic activities are included in national income measurement.

✅ Included in National Income

  • Market goods and services
  • Goods produced for own final consumption
  • Owner-occupied housing (imputed rent)
  • Paid domestic staff services
  • Agricultural crops grown for own consumption

❌ Excluded from National Income

  • Unpaid household work (e.g. cooking, cleaning by family members)
  • Child care (unpaid)
  • Natural resources without human intervention (e.g. wild forests, mineral deposits not yet extracted)
  • Leisure activities
📝
Key Principle
Only economic activities that involve a monetary transaction or imputed value are included within the production boundary.
🔍

Non-Observed Economic Activities

Activities that are not captured in official statistics but still involve production of goods and services.

🕵️ Hidden Economy
  • Legal economic activities
  • Concealed from authorities to avoid:
  • • Tax payments
  • • Regulatory compliance
  • • Social security contributions
  • Also called the underground or shadow economy
🚫 Illegal Activities
  • Activities forbidden by law
  • Or unauthorized production
  • • Drug trafficking
  • • Human trafficking
  • • Illegal gambling
  • • Production without license
📊

Three Approaches to GDP

GDP can be measured in three different ways, all of which yield the same result in equilibrium.

🏭 Product Approach
  • Value added in Agriculture
  • + Value added in Industry
  • + Value added in Services
  • + Net taxes on products
  • = GDP at market prices
💵 Income Approach
  • Compensation of employees
  • + Gross operational surplus
  • + Mixed income
  • + Other net taxes on production
  • = GDP at market prices
💲 Expenditure Approach
  • Consumption (C)
  • + Investment (I)
  • + Government spending (G)
  • + Net Exports (X − M)
  • = GDP at market prices
Expenditure Approach GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
Product Approach GDP = Σ Value Added + Net Taxes
Income Approach GDP = Employee Compensation + Operating Surplus + Mixed Income + Net Taxes

GDP vs GNP

🏴 GDP
  • Gross Domestic Product
  • Total value of goods & services produced within a country's borders
  • Includes output by foreign-owned firms operating domestically
  • Excludes output by domestic firms operating abroad
🌍 GNP
  • Gross National Product
  • Total value of goods & services produced by citizens/residents of a country
  • Includes output by domestic firms operating abroad
  • Excludes output by foreign-owned firms domestically
Relationship GNP = GDP + Net primary income from rest of the world
Net Primary Income Net Primary Income = Income earned by residents abroad − Income earned by non-residents domestically
➖

Gross vs Net

➕ Gross
  • Includes depreciation (fixed capital consumption)
  • GDP = Gross Domestic Product
  • Gross Investment includes replacement capital
➖ Net
  • Excludes depreciation
  • NDP = Net Domestic Product
  • NDP = GDP − Fixed Capital Consumption
  • Net Investment = Gross Investment − Depreciation
Net Domestic Product NDP = GDP − Fixed Capital Consumption (Depreciation)

Factor Incomes

Factor incomes represent the earnings by factors of production in the economy.

🧑‍🏫
Compensation of Employees
Wages, salaries, bonuses, and employer social contributions
🏠
Rental Income
Income from land and property (actual & imputed)
💵
Net Interest Income
Interest earned − interest paid on capital
🏦
Corporate Profits
Profits after tax, dividends, retained earnings
🧩
Self-Employment / Mixed Income
Income of unincorporated enterprises (cannot separate labour from capital)

Final Product vs Value Added

The value added approach avoids double counting by summing only the additional value created at each stage of production.

Stage 1: Farmer grows wheat       Value Added = $100
                     |
Stage 2: Miller grinds flour      Value Added = $50
                     |
Stage 3: Baker bakes bread        Value Added = $50
                     |
Stage 4: Retailer sells bread     Value Added = $30
                     |
            Total Value Added = $230  (GDP contribution)
      (vs. Final Price = $230 — same result!)
⚠️
Why Value Added?
If we summed all sales (100+150+200+230 = $680), we would overcount — double counting the value of intermediate goods. Value added avoids this.

Importance & Limitations of National Accounting

⭐ Importance / Uses

  • Assess the overall performance of the economy
  • Measure economic growth over time (real GDP)
  • Compute per capita GDP for standard of living comparisons
  • International comparisons of economic output
  • Basis for government policy formulation (fiscal & monetary)
  • Input for business investment decisions

⚠️ Limitations / Criticisms

🧑‍🏭
Unpaid Household Work
Excluded from GDP despite having economic value
🕵️
Informal / Shadow Economy
Hidden and illegal activities not captured
🏞️
Environmental Effects
GDP ignores resource depletion and pollution
🎶
Leisure
Reduction in leisure time is not accounted for

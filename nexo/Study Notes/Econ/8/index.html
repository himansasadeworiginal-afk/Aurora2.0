Market Failure

Definition: The inability of the market mechanism to distribute scarce resources toward society's wellbeing.

Reasons for Market Failure

  • Inefficiency of resource allocation — markets fail to produce the socially optimal quantity of goods
  • Macroeconomic instability — fluctuations in aggregate demand cause inflation and unemployment
  • Unequal income distribution — markets produce inequitable outcomes based on labour, education, property, and luck

Inefficiency of Resource Allocation

  • Non-provision of public goods
  • Non-optimal consumption of quasi-public goods
  • Non-provision of welfare goods optimally
  • Provision of demerit goods
  • Over-utilization of common resources
  • Externalities
  • Imperfect competition / monopoly
  • Imperfect information

Macroeconomic Instability

  • Fluctuations in aggregate demand, especially private investment
  • Inflation — rising general price level reduces purchasing power
  • Unemployment — resources, especially labour, remain idle

Markets alone cannot self-correct quickly; government intervention through fiscal and monetary policy is often required to stabilize the economy.

Role of Government

1. Efficient Allocation

Providing public goods, quasi-public goods, welfare goods, and limiting demerit goods.

2. Fair Distribution

Redistribution of income and wealth, limits on wealth accumulation, land reform.

3. Macroeconomic Stability

Managing aggregate demand to control inflation and unemployment.

4. Growth & Development

Promoting economic growth and sustainable development through policy.

Externalities

Externalities: Non-compensated benefits or costs to third parties arising from production or consumption.

4 Types of Externalities

Negative Production

Pollution from coal plants, pesticide runoff

Remedy: Environmental taxes, licenses, fines

Positive Production

Train station shelter, new technology spillovers

Remedy: Subsidies, internalization

Negative Consumption

Smoking, loud music, noise pollution

Remedy: Taxes, fines, regulation

Positive Consumption

Good architecture, education, healthy diet

Remedy: Subsidies, public provision

Remedies for Externalities

Environmental taxes Licenses Subsidies Internalization Bargaining (Coase) Fines

Objectives of Taxation

Raise revenue Redistribute income Stabilize economy Discourage demerit goods Protect domestic industries Correct externalities

Principles of Taxation

Equity

Horizontal: Equal treatment of equals
Vertical: Unequal treatment of unequals

Certainty

Taxpayers should know when, how, and how much

Economical

Cost of collection should be low relative to revenue

Efficiency

Minimize distortion to economic decisions

Convenience

Ease of payment for taxpayers

Flexibility

Ability to adjust to changing conditions

Benefit vs Ability-to-Pay Principle

Benefit Principle

Taxes paid should reflect benefits received from public services.

Ability-to-Pay Principle

Taxes should be based on the taxpayer's capacity to pay, regardless of benefits received.

Expansionary vs Contractionary Budgetary Policy

Expansionary

Increase spending / cut taxes to stimulate the economy during a recession.

Contractionary

Decrease spending / raise taxes to cool an overheating economy and control inflation.

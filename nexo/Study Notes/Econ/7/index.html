1 Price Concepts

🔹 Absolute Price

The nominal price of a good expressed in monetary units — what you actually pay at the store. Also called money price.

🔹 Relative Price

The price of one good compared to another; represents the opportunity cost of consuming one good in terms of the other.

Relative Price = Price of Good A ÷ Price of Good B

🔹 General Price Level

The average of all prices in an economy, measured via a price index. A rise in the general price level = inflation.

2 Price Index & Inflation

🔹 Price Index

A measure of the average price level of a basket of goods and services relative to a base year. Examples: CPI, GDP deflator.

Price Index = (Cost of Basket in Current Year ÷ Cost of Basket in Base Year) × 100

4 Causes of Inflation

📈 Demand-Pull Inflation

"Too much money chasing too few goods." Aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply at full employment.

Quantity Theory of Money (QTM)

MV = PT
M = Money Supply | V = Velocity | P = Price Level | T = Transactions

If V and T are constant, an increase in M causes a proportionate increase in P (inflation).

Keynesian Theory

When aggregate demand exceeds full-employment output, the excess demand pulls prices upward. Output cannot rise further, so the entire increase in AD translates into higher prices.

📉 Cost-Push Inflation

An increase in the cost of inputs (wages, raw materials, energy) shifts the Aggregate Supply curve leftward, raising the price level.

  • Organised labour (wage push) — unions negotiate higher wages → firms raise prices to maintain margins.
  • Oligopoly firms (profit push) — firms with market power raise prices to increase profit margins.

6 Money

📌 Definition

Money is any asset that is generally accepted as payment for goods and services and for settling debts.

❌ Drawbacks of the Barter System

  • Lack of double coincidence of wants — both parties must want what the other offers.
  • No common measure of value — no standard unit to express prices.
  • Indivisibility — some goods cannot be split into smaller units.
  • Deferred payments — difficult to make credit/loan contracts.
  • Storing value — most goods perish or depreciate.

✅ Characteristics of Good Money

  • General acceptance — everyone trusts and accepts it.
  • Durability — lasts through repeated use.
  • Uniformity — identical units have identical value.
  • Divisibility — can be split into smaller denominations.
  • Portability — easy to carry and transport.
  • Stability of value — does not fluctuate wildly.
  • Legal validity — recognised by law as legal tender.
  • Difficulty to counterfeit — secure against forgery.
  • Easy identification — recognisable by the public.

8 Demand for Money

🔹 Liquidity Preference Theory (Keynes)

People hold money for three motives:

MotivePurposeRelationship with IncomeRelationship with Interest Rate
TransactionEveryday purchasesPositive (↑ income → ↑ demand)Inelastic
PrecautionaryUnexpected expensesPositiveInelastic
SpeculativeTo exploit future interest rate changes—Inverse (↓ interest → ↑ demand)

📊 Factors Affecting Money Demand

  • Real income — higher income → more transactions → higher money demand.
  • Interest rate — higher rate → higher opportunity cost of holding money → lower demand.
  • Price level — higher prices → more money needed for same transactions.
  • Institutional factors — payment frequency, banking habits.
  • Financial innovations — ATMs, mobile banking reduce money demand.

10 Financial System of Sri Lanka

🏛 Components

  • Financial institutions — banks, insurance, finance companies, pension funds.
  • Financial markets — money market, capital market, foreign exchange market.
  • Financial instruments — shares, bonds, T-bills, insurance policies.
  • Financial infrastructure — payments, clearing, settlement systems.
  • Regulatory bodies — CBSL, SEC, IRCSL.

🏦 Central Bank of Sri Lanka (CBSL)

  • Established: 1950 (as the Central Bank of Ceylon).
  • First Governor: John Exter.

Objectives

  • Economic & price stability — stable inflation, sustainable growth.
  • Financial system stability — sound banking & financial infrastructure.

Main Functions

  • Formulating & implementing monetary policy.
  • Formulating & implementing exchange rate policy.
  • Issuing currency (sole authority).
  • Managing foreign reserves.
  • Acting as banker to the government.

Agency Functions

  • Public debt management — issuing & servicing government securities.
  • Foreign exchange control — administering exchange controls.
  • EPF management — Employees' Provident Fund administration.
11 Monetary Policy

🔹 Frameworks

  • Monetary targeting — targeting growth rate of a money aggregate (e.g., M2b).
  • Flexible inflation targeting — targeting a specific inflation range while considering output stability.

📊 How SRR Works

The Statutory Reserve Ratio (SRR) is the percentage of deposits banks must keep with the central bank. Changing SRR directly impacts lending capacity.

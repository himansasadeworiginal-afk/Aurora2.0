Protectionism

Protectionism refers to government policies that restrict international trade in order to protect domestic industries from foreign competition.

📜
Definition
Government policies restricting international trade to help domestic industries
🏭
Goal
Protect domestic producers from foreign competition
⚖️
Two Types
Tariff Protectionism & Non-Tariff Barriers
💳

Tariff Protectionism

Tariff protectionism involves imposing tariffs (taxes) on imports and exports. The main objective is to protect domestic producers from foreign competition by making imported goods more expensive.

Foreign goods cheaper → Govt imposes tariff → Imported price increases → Domestic goods relatively cheaper

Example

💰 How Tariffs Make Domestic Products Cheaper Relative to Imports

Before Tariff

Domestic Product: LKR 3,000 | Foreign Product: LKR 2,500 → Foreign is cheaper

After Tariff

Tariff on foreign product: +LKR 2,000

Foreign Product becomes: LKR 4,500

➡ Now domestic (LKR 3,000) is cheaper than foreign (LKR 4,500)

Tariffs can be imposed on both imports and exports, though import tariffs are far more common for protectionist purposes.
🔢

Nominal Rate of Protection

The nominal rate of protection is the percentage tariff imposed on a product as it enters the country. It reflects the tariff rate applied to the final product without considering imported inputs.

Nominal Rate = (Tariff ÷ Original Price) × 100
📍 Example

20% tariff on clothing

A 20% tariff on imported clothing means the nominal rate of protection is 20%.

However, this does not show the actual protection received by the domestic producer, because the producer may use imported inputs/intermediate goods that are also subject to tariffs.

⚠️ Limitation: The nominal rate ignores the effect of tariffs on imported inputs used in domestic production. This is where the Effective Rate of Protection (ERP) becomes useful.
📊

Effective Rate of Protection

The Effective Rate of Protection (ERP) measures the actual protection received by the domestic producer by considering tariffs on both the final product and imported inputs.

ERP = (Vi - Vo) / Vo × 100
Vi
Value Added After Tariff
Value added when tariffs are applied to final product and inputs
Vo
Value Added Before Tariff
Value added under free trade (no tariffs)

Worked Example 1: Tariff on Final Product Only

Given

Price of Shoes = Rs. 5,000

Cost of Inputs = Rs. 3,000

Value Added (Vo) = Rs. 5,000 - Rs. 3,000 = Rs. 2,000

After 10% Tariff on Final Product

New Imported Price = Rs. 5,000 + 10% = Rs. 5,500

New Value Added (Vi) = Rs. 5,500 - Rs. 3,000 = Rs. 2,500

ERP Calculation

ERP = (2,500 - 2,000) / 2,000 × 100 = 25%

Worked Example 2: Tariff on Both Final Product and Inputs

Same as above + 5% tariff on inputs

Input cost after 5% tariff = Rs. 3,000 + 5% = Rs. 3,150

New Value Added (Vi) = Rs. 5,500 - Rs. 3,150 = Rs. 2,350

ERP Calculation

ERP = (2,350 - 2,000) / 2,000 × 100 = 17.5%

Key Determinants of ERP

↗️
Higher ERP when
Higher nominal tariff on final product
↘️
Higher ERP when
Lower tariff on imported inputs
▲
Higher ERP when
Higher % of imported inputs in production

🔢 Nominal Rate
  • Tariff % on final product only
  • Ignores imported inputs
  • Simple tariff rate
  • Less accurate measure of protection
📊 Effective Rate (ERP)
  • Considers tariffs on final product + inputs
  • Accounts for imported inputs
  • Measures actual value added change
  • More accurate measure of protection

Non-Tariff Barriers

Non-tariff barriers are trade restrictions other than tariffs used to restrict imports and protect domestic industries.

🎛️
Quotas
Quantitative limits on imports
💰
Subsidies / Incentives
Financial support to domestic producers
💩
Dumping
Selling goods below cost abroad
🚫
Voluntary Export Restraints
Exporter "voluntarily" limits exports
💱
Foreign Exchange Controls
Limiting access to foreign currency
🥩
Rationing
Limiting quantity available to consumers
⛔
Trade Embargoes
Complete ban on trade with a country
📋
Administrative / Technical Standards
Complex regulations, inspections, certifications

Comparison: Tariff vs Non-Tariff Barriers

💳 Tariff Barriers
  • Taxes on imports/exports
  • Generates government revenue
  • Transparent and measurable
  • Price-based restriction
  • Easier to negotiate/reduce in trade agreements
🚧 Non-Tariff Barriers
  • Quantity/regulation-based restrictions
  • No direct revenue generation
  • Often opaque and complex
  • Quantity/standard-based restriction
  • Harder to quantify and negotiate
🌍

Foreign Investments

Foreign investment refers to the flow of capital from one country to another for productive or financial purposes.

Types of Foreign Investments

🏭
FDI (Foreign Direct Investment)
Acquiring productive real assets with controlling ownership & management involvement
📈
Portfolio Investments
Purchasing financial assets (shares, securities, commercial papers) without control/management

Comparison: FDI vs Portfolio Investment

🏭 FDI
  • Long-term investment
  • Involves control & management
  • Acquires real/physical assets
  • Less liquid
  • Transfers technology & management skills
  • Creates employment directly
📈 Portfolio Investment
  • Short- to medium-term investment
  • No control or management
  • Acquires financial assets
  • Highly liquid
  • No technology transfer
  • No direct employment creation

Recent FDI Trends in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka has attracted FDI in sectors such as telecommunications, apparel, tourism, ports, and infrastructure. Key sources include China, India, Singapore, and the UK. The government has established the Board of Investment (BOI) to facilitate and promote FDI.

Effects of FDI

✅ Favourable Effects
  • Improves resource allocation efficiency
  • Fills the resource gap (savings-investment gap)
  • Fills the foreign exchange gap
  • Helps solve BOP problems
  • Does not increase national debt
  • Brings new technology & management techniques
❌ Unfavourable Effects
  • Outflow of dividends, interest & profits
  • May harm domestic economic activities
  • Loss of employment in domestic firms
  • Overconsumption of natural resources
  • Collapse of domestic industries due to competition
  • Tax revenue issues (transfer pricing)
  • Outflow of funds for domestic investors
  • Brain drain of skilled workers

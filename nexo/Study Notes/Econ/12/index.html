Overview

Since gaining independence from Britain in 1948, Sri Lanka's economy has undergone dramatic transformations — from a colonial plantation-based export economy to a closed import-substituting regime, and then to a liberalized export-oriented economy, and most recently a services-driven post-war economy facing new challenges.

Timeline of Economic Policy

1948 – 1960
🌍 Open Economy · Plantation Exports
Continued colonial trade patterns. Tea, rubber, coconut dominated exports. Minimal state intervention. Relatively open trade regime.
1960 – 1977
🔒 Closed Economy · Import Substitution
Nationalization of key industries (banks, insurance, plantations). High tariffs, import controls. State-led industrialization. ISI strategy adopted.
1977 – 2009
💰 Liberalization · Export Orientation
1977 economic reforms under J.R. Jayewardene. Removed import controls, floated currency, privatized state enterprises. EPZs established. Garment industry boom.
2009 – 2019
🏗️ Post-War Development Boom
End of civil war (2009). Infrastructure boom: highways, ports, airports. Tourism surge. Construction-driven growth. Rising debt.
2020 – Present
⚠️ Debt Crisis · IMF · Recovery
COVID-19 hit tourism. 2022 economic crisis: sovereign default, inflation, shortages. IMF bailout program (2023). Ongoing restructuring.
🔒

Import Substitution Industrialization

ISI was the dominant strategy from the 1960s to 1977, aimed at reducing dependence on imported manufactured goods by promoting domestic industries behind protective barriers.

Characteristics of ISI in Sri Lanka

  • Protectionist policies: High tariffs and quantitative import controls
  • State-led industrialization: Public sector dominated manufacturing
  • Nationalization: Banks, insurance, plantations, petroleum, transport
  • Overvalued currency: Made imports cheaper, exports costlier
  • Licensing system: Strict import licensing and foreign exchange controls

Rationale for ISI

🧱
Infant Industry
Protect young domestic industries until competitive
💰
Save Foreign Exchange
Reduce import bill by producing locally
🏢
Industrialization
Shift from colonial plantation mono-economy
👥
Self-Sufficiency
Reduce dependence on former colonial powers
Effective Rate of Protection (ERP) = (Value Added in World Prices − Value Added in Domestic Prices) ÷ Value Added in World Prices × 100

Export-Oriented Industrial Policy

The landmark 1977 reforms shifted Sri Lanka from ISI to an export-oriented strategy, opening the economy to global markets.

Key Reforms of 1977

  • Unified and floated the exchange rate
  • Abolished most import controls and reduced tariffs
  • Removed price controls
  • Privatization of state-owned enterprises
  • Established Export Processing Zones (EPZs) — Katunayake, Biyagama, Koggala
  • Liberalized foreign direct investment (FDI)

Export Processing Zones

  • Katunayake EPZ (1978) — first and largest, near airport
  • Biyagama EPZ — near Colombo
  • Koggala EPZ — southern Sri Lanka
  • BOI (Board of Investment) — facilitates FDI with tax incentives

ISI vs Export-Oriented Strategy

🔒 Import Substitution
  • Protect domestic industries via tariffs
  • State-owned enterprises dominate
  • Overvalued currency
  • Import licensing & exchange controls
  • Limited foreign investment
  • Focus on domestic market
  • Result: inefficiency, high costs, foreign exchange shortages
💰 Export-Oriented
  • Remove trade barriers, open markets
  • Private sector-led growth
  • Market-determined exchange rate
  • Liberalized trade and capital flows
  • FDI encouraged via BOI incentives
  • Focus on global competitiveness
  • Result: rapid export growth, but import dependence
📅

Pre-1977 vs Post-1977 Economy

💺 Pre-1977
  • Trade: Closed, protectionist
  • Exchange rate: Overvalued, fixed
  • State role: Dominant, nationalized industries
  • Industry: Infant, inefficient, high-cost
  • Agriculture: Plantations + subsistence
  • Growth: Slow (< 3% average)
  • Inflation: Suppressed via controls
  • FDI: Heavily restricted
📈 Post-1977
  • Trade: Open, liberalized
  • Exchange rate: Floating, market-determined
  • State role: Reduced, privatization
  • Industry: Competitive, export-oriented
  • Agriculture: Commercialization + diversification
  • Growth: Faster (5–6% pre-war)
  • Inflation: Market-driven, periodic spikes
  • FDI: Encouraged via BOI incentives
🏛️

Structural Changes in the Economy

Sri Lanka has undergone a major structural transformation from an agriculture-based economy to a services-dominated economy over the post-independence period.

Sectoral Composition of GDP (Approximate)

1960 — Agriculture Dominant

🌾 Agriculture 38% 🏭 Industry 20% 🏢 Services 42%

1980 — Industry Rising

🌾 Agriculture 28% 🏭 Industry 30% 🏢 Services 42%

2020 — Services Dominant

🌾 Agriculture ~8% 🏭 Industry ~27% 🏢 Services ~65%
Agriculture Industry Services

Key Structural Changes

  • Agriculture → Services: Agriculture's share fell from ~38% (1960) to ~8% (2020)
  • Industry: Rose to ~30% after liberalization, then stabilized at ~27%
  • Services: Expanded from ~42% to ~65%, driven by finance, telecom, tourism, IT
  • Urbanization: Western Province (Colombo) dominates economic activity
  • Labour force: Shift from agriculture to industry and services; remittance-driven migration

Recent Challenges

💵
Debt Crisis
Sovereign default in 2022. External debt ~$50B. Restructuring underway with IMF.
📈
Inflation
Peaked at ~70% in 2022. Caused by money printing, supply shocks, currency collapse.
🏛️
IMF Program
$3B Extended Fund Facility (2023). Conditions: tax reforms, SOE reform, fiscal discipline.
🌍
External Sector
Remittances down, tourism recovery slow, terms of trade deterioration.

Root Causes of 2022 Crisis

  • Tax cuts: Large tax cuts in 2019 reduced government revenue sharply
  • Money printing: Central bank financed fiscal deficits, triggering inflation
  • Foreign exchange shortage: Tourism collapse (COVID) + remittance drop + import bill
  • Debt unsustainability: High borrowing for infrastructure with low returns
  • Organic farming ban: 2021 chemical fertilizer ban devastated tea/rubber yields

Exploring sole proprietorships, partnerships, companies, co-operatives, franchises, business combinations, and the public sector.

■ What is an Organization?

A social unit made up with a group of individuals joining together and having a common goal is an organization.

Examples: School, Temple, Church, Shop

Common Characteristics of a Formal Organization

  • Has a goal
  • Involves individuals
  • Has an organizational structure
Business organizations are enterprises engaged in production, distribution and selling of goods and services with the objective of earning profits or achieving social wellbeing.

Business organizations can be categorized as private sector and public sector based on ownership.

☷ Sole Proprietorship

Where the ownership of the business is held by a single individual, it is called sole proprietorship.

Examples: Chanuli Super Centre, Dasatha Book shop, Imalka saloon

  • The owner provides the capital required on his own
  • All managerial decisions are made by the owner
  • The unlimited liability of the owner
  • Not a legal entity
  • Registration is not compulsory
  • Enjoy profits and losses alone
  • Faces risks alone
  • No continuity
  • Accounting is not compulsory
  • Not liable for the taxes in the name of the business
  • Easy to initiate
  • All the profits belong to the owner
  • Ability of independent decision making
  • Fewer rules and regulations are applicable
  • The ability of keeping the business information confidentially
  • Termination of the business is convenient
  • Possibility of maintaining direct relations with the customers
  • Control is convenient
  • The unlimited liability of the owner
  • Problems increase when additional capital is needed
  • Possibility of unsuccessful decisions by the owner
  • Not a legal entity
  • No continuity
  • Management becomes inconvenient with expansion
  • Bearing losses alone

If a sole proprietorship is carried on in a name other than the full name of the owner, registration is compulsory at the Provincial Business Name Registrar under the Business Name Ordinance No. 6 of 1918.

  1. Obtain an application form from the Divisional Secretariat office
  2. Obtain a report from the relevant Grama Niladari
  3. Submit the completed form, Grama Niladari report, and fee to the Divisional Secretary
  4. Certificate of registration is issued after examination

❐ Partnership

Partnership Ordinance 1890: “The relationship between the individuals who carry out a business with a profit motive is Partnership.”

Examples: Gunawardena & Sons, Don Carolis & Sons

Essential Characteristics

  • Profit generating objectives
  • The existence of business activity
  • Existence of individuals
  • Having a mutual relationship
  • Minimum 2 partners, maximum 20 partners
  • There must be an agreement between the partners
  • Having an agreement on profit sharing
  • Every partner is an agent and a head of himself and other partners
  • The existence of any kind of business affair

The written agreement between partners is known as the partnership deed or partnership constitution. It includes:

  • Basic info: business name, partner details, nature and duration
  • Capital investment details
  • Profit and loss sharing arrangement
  • Objectives of the partnership
  • Limitations on drawings
  • Interest on capital and drawings
  • Conditions on dissolution
  • Additional capital investment conditions
  • Actions on admission, retirement, or death of a partner
  • Expenditure incurred personally on behalf of the partnership must be reimbursed
  • Partners are entitled to 5% interest on money invested beyond agreed capital
  • No partner is entitled to interest on capital (standard)
  • Every partner has the right to participate in management
  • No partner has the right to receive a salary for services rendered
  • New partners require consent of all existing partners
  • Disagreements solved by majority; fundamental changes require unanimous agreement
  • Books of accounts kept at head office; any partner may inspect and copy

Advantages

  • Easy to commence
  • Can be dissolved easily
  • Possibility of gathering immense capital
  • Multiple skills and talents
  • Liabilities distributed among partners
  • Enhanced enthusiasm and efficiency

Limitations

  • Unlimited liability of partners
  • Disagreements can adversely affect the business
  • No continuity
  • All partners liable for conduct of a single partner

◦ Company

A collection of individuals incorporated under the Companies Act No. 7 of 2007 can be introduced as a company.

Examples: Aralia Company Ltd, Pratheeba Company (Pvt.) Ltd

  • Independent entity incorporated under the Company Act
  • Independent legal entity distinct from its shareholders
  • Continuous existence / perpetual succession
  • Liability of members/shareholders is limited
  • Capital accumulation through share issues
  • Ability to transfer shares
  • Management by a Board of Directors
  • Ability to raise a large amount of capital
  • Possibility of beginning large-scale businesses
  • Ability to recruit a smart management team
  • Ability to attract new investors (limited liability)
  • Death/resignation of a shareholder does not affect the business
  • Ability to assemble qualified and skillful human resources
  • The business has a legality
  • Legal impacts being strict
  • Establishment procedure is complicated and expensive
  • Minority shareholders lack influence (1 vote per share)
  • Profit subjected to double taxation (company profit + dividends)
  • Dissolution procedure is complicated

Incorporation is done under Companies Act No. 7 of 2007 with the Department of Company Registrar.

  1. Register the name of the company
  2. Submit documents: application for registration, articles of association, director consent statements, secretary consent statement
  3. The Articles of Association contains objectives, shareholder rights and responsibilities, management and administration details
  4. Registrar issues the Certificate of Incorporation once satisfied

☰ Co-Operative Business

“A co-operative is an autonomous association of people united voluntarily in their common economic, social and cultural needs and aspirations through a jointly owned and democratically controlled enterprise.”
— International Co-operative Alliance

Examples: Maharagama Multi-purpose Co-operative Society Ltd, Cloth-weavers’ Co-operative Society Ltd, Fisheries Co-operative Society Ltd

  • An independent business organization
  • Group of individuals who have got together voluntarily
  • Collective ownership of its members
  • Having common needs and expectations
  • Democratically controlled business
  • Value of a share is unchanged (Rs. 100)
  • One vote for each member (one man one vote)
  • Surplus distributed according to transaction value with the society
  • Profit making is not the initial objective
  • Voluntary and open membership
  • Democratic control of members
  • Member economic participation
  • Autonomy and independence
  • Co-operative education, training and provision of information
  • Co-operation among co-operatives (regional, national, international)
  • Concern for the community

Benefits

  • 10 people can initiate conveniently at low investment
  • Democratic control
  • Goods and services at reasonable prices
  • Different types of business activities
  • Mutual co-operation
  • Surplus distributed among members

Limitations

  • Limited means of fund raising
  • Restricted to co-operative policies
  • Limited opening hours
  • Public sector interference
  • No credit sales
  • Board may be inefficient (elected by voting)

Registered under Co-operative Societies Act No. 05 of 1972 with the Co-operative Development Commissioner.

  1. Any 10 people above 18 may apply
  2. Submit: application form, 2 copies of interim constitution, feasibility report, minutes of the general meeting, membership fee certificate, route map
  3. Commissioner inspects and confirms compliance
  4. Certificate of registration is issued

Emerging Trends

  • Modified self-service outlets (Co-op City, Co-op Super)
  • New business fields (insurance, fuel stations)
  • Electronic payment systems
  • Mobile shopping centers

Development Proposals

  • Extend business hours
  • Credit sales options
  • Experienced management board
  • Promotional strategies
  • Expand self-service outlets
  • Attractive showrooms
  • Increase member benefits
  • Member promotion propaganda
  • Modern technology for consumer service

★ Franchise

A business model where one firm (the franchisor) assigns the authority of selling its goods/services to another (the franchisee) in a specific trade zone.
  • Franchisor — the franchising company that permits the franchisee to market products under its brand
  • Franchisee — the organization that receives permission from the franchisor

The Franchise Agreement governs the relationship, including use of trade name, logo, brand, rules, services, financial conditions, initial payment, and monthly royalties.

Franchisor Provides

  • Permission to use trade name
  • Management training
  • Marketing assistance
  • Financial assistance
  • Layout & design help
  • Product supply & equipment
  • Technical knowledge

Franchee Must

  • Act per franchisor conditions
  • Make capital investment
  • Purchase only from franchisor/authorized suppliers
  • Make agreed payments
TypeDescriptionExample
Product FranchiseFranchisee authorized to purchase and resell goods under brand nameIOC Fuel Stations
Manufacturing FranchiseFranchisee authorized to manufacture and distribute under brand nameCoca-Cola, Pepsi bottling
Business Format FranchiseFranchisee follows franchisor’s entire business procedure and brandMcDonalds, KFC, Pizza Hut

Benefits to Franchisor

  • Expand without further investment
  • Share advertising costs
  • Returns from franchisee investment
  • Spontaneous returns from franchisee efficiency
  • Nourished by the franchisee

Benefits to Franchisee

  • Large market niche from goodwill
  • Lower tendency to be unsuccessful
  • Convenient publicity (popular trade name)
  • Management training
  • Financial support
  • Raw materials at minimum price

Limitations for Franchisor

  • Franchisee failure affects profits
  • Unfavorable activities affect goodwill
  • Franchisee retains most profits

Limitations for Franchisee

  • No perfect autonomy
  • Greater cost to receive franchise
  • Profitability uncertainty
  • Dependence on franchisor’s goodwill
  • Inconvenience from agreement adherence

⊕ Business Combinations

Joining up of two or more businesses and engaging in business activities can be introduced as Business Combinations.

Merger (Amalgamation)

  • Two or more companies convert into a single entity
  • One company purchases assets and liabilities of another
  • Or all companies terminate and form a new company

Acquisition

  • One company purchases most ordinary share capital of another
  • The purchasing company gains power of control
TypeDefinitionExamples
HorizontalCombination of businesses engaged in similar activitiesTwo footwear manufacturers; MBSL Savings + Merchant Bank + MBSL Financial (2015); Microsoft + Nokia; Fair First Insurance amalgamation; DFCC Bank merger
VerticalCombination of businesses at different stages in the same production/marketing processShoe manufacturer + leather manufacturer + shoe marketing company
ConglomerateCombination of businesses not in the same industryShoe manufacturer + biscuit manufacturer

☂ Public Sector / State Sector

All business entities carried out and controlled by the state or a local authority are known as state sector or public sector enterprises.
  • To control pricing levels and prevent unreasonable private profits
  • To prevent monopolistic firms
  • To engage in fields required for national defense
  • To provide essential goods and services at reasonable prices
  • To minimize wastage of natural resources
  • To maintain non-profit large-scale projects
